From Sunlight to Watts

A First-Principles Derivation of Solar Panel Power Output

Every hour, 430 quintillion joules of solar energy reach Earth's surface — enough to power human civilisation for an entire year. But how much of that bounty can a single rooftop panel actually capture? And how does the answer shift with latitude, tilt angle, and season? Starting from nuclear fusion in the Sun's core, we trace every energy transformation — across 150 million kilometres of space, through the atmosphere, and down to the kilowatt-hours on your electricity meter.

Let's build the calculation layer by layer — each step adds exactly one physical idea and one new equation.

Step 1

The Sun as a Power Source

From nuclear fusion to the Solar Constant

Everything begins in the Sun's core, where the proton–proton chain fuses hydrogen nuclei into helium. A tiny fraction of the input mass — about 0.7% — is converted into energy via Einstein's $E = \Delta m\,c^2$. That seemingly small fraction means roughly four million tonnes of matter vanish every second, reborn as light and heat.

The result is the Sun's luminosity — the total power it radiates in all directions:

$$L_\odot = 3.828 \times 10^{26} \text{ W}$$

This staggering number floods outward uniformly across all directions. At any distance $d$, that power is spread over the surface of an ever-growing sphere:

$$I(d) = \frac{L_\odot}{4\pi d^2}$$

This is the inverse-square law — one of the most universal relationships in physics. Double the distance, and the brightness drops to a quarter.

Plugging in Earth's mean distance of 1 Astronomical Unit (AU $= 1.496 \times 10^{11}$ m) gives us the Solar Constant:

$$S_0 = \frac{L_\odot}{4\pi\,\text{AU}^2} \approx 1361 \text{ W/m}^2$$

$S_0$ is the power density arriving at a surface perpendicular to the Sun's rays, just outside Earth's atmosphere. Think of it as the "raw input" before the atmosphere takes its cut — and it is the single most important number in all of solar energy.

Intuition check: 1361 W/m² is roughly the output of a household hair dryer — delivered to every single square metre facing the Sun, from 150 million kilometres away. The Sun's generosity is remarkable.

Step 2

Earth's Elliptical Orbit

How the Earth–Sun distance varies over the year

We said $S_0$ assumes Earth's mean distance. But Earth's orbit is not a perfect circle — it is a slight ellipse, as Kepler discovered four centuries ago. The eccentricity is small ($e = 0.0167$, just 1.7%), yet it modulates the top-of-atmosphere irradiance by a noticeable $\pm 3.4\%$ over the year.

Since irradiance scales as $d^{-2}$, we define the extraterrestrial irradiance (ETR):

$$G_{\text{ext}}(n) = S_0 \cdot E_0(n)$$

where $E_0(n)$ is the eccentricity correction factor. Spencer (1971) provides a compact harmonic approximation:

$$E_0 = 1.000110 + 0.034221\cos B + 0.001280\sin B + 0.000719\cos 2B + 0.000077\sin 2B$$

with the day angle $B = 2\pi(n-1)/365.25$ in radians, where $n$ is the day of the year (January 1 = day 1).

Here is a counterintuitive fact: Earth is closest to the Sun in early January — the depth of Northern-Hemisphere winter. This is a vivid reminder that seasons are caused by Earth's tilt, not its distance.

Step 3

Earth's Axial Tilt and the Seasons

Deriving the solar declination angle

If distance doesn't cause the seasons, what does? The answer is tilt. Earth's spin axis is inclined by $\varepsilon = 23.44{^\circ}$ relative to the plane of its orbit. As Earth circles the Sun, this tilt stays fixed in space (pointing roughly toward Polaris), so the Sun appears to migrate north and south across the sky over the course of a year.

The declination angle $\delta$ captures this motion: it is the latitude at which the Sun stands directly overhead at solar noon. It sweeps from $+23.44{^\circ}$ at the summer solstice (around June 21) through $0{^\circ}$ at the equinoxes to $-23.44{^\circ}$ at the winter solstice (around December 21).

The angle swept from the vernal equinox (around March 21, day $n_0 \approx 81$) is:

$$\psi = \frac{360{^\circ}}{365.25}\,(n - 81)$$

Projecting Earth's tilted axis gives us a simple approximation:

$$\delta \approx 23.44{^\circ}\sin\left(\frac{360{^\circ}}{365.25}(n-81)\right)$$

For higher accuracy, the Spencer (1971) harmonic series is preferred:

$$\delta = 0.006918 - 0.399912\cos B + 0.070257\sin B - 0.006758\cos 2B + 0.000907\sin 2B - 0.002697\cos 3B + 0.001480\sin 3B$$

Declination is the astronomical equivalent of latitude for the Sun — it tells us how high the Sun can climb at noon on any given day.

Step 4

The Sun's Path Across Your Sky

Solar altitude and azimuth from spherical trigonometry

Knowing the declination is only half the story — we also need the Sun's position at every hour, not just noon. We work in the local horizontal coordinate system: altitude $\alpha$ (angle above the horizon) and azimuth $A_z$ (compass direction, measured clockwise from North).

Three ingredients combine to pin down the Sun's position at any moment:

  • Latitude $\varphi$: your location on Earth
  • Declination $\delta(n)$: the Sun's celestial latitude (Step 3)
  • Hour angle $\omega$: how far the Sun is from solar noon. Earth rotates at 15°/hour, so $\omega = 15{^\circ} \times (T_{\text{solar}} - 12)$

Applying the spherical law of cosines to the triangle formed by the zenith, the celestial pole, and the Sun yields the master formula of solar geometry:

$$\sin\alpha = \sin\varphi\sin\delta + \cos\varphi\cos\delta\cos\omega$$

Given your latitude $\varphi$, today's declination $\delta$, and the current hour angle $\omega$, this single equation is enough to locate the Sun in your sky.

Sunrise and sunset occur when $\alpha = 0$, giving the sunrise hour angle:

$$\omega_s = \arccos(-\tan\varphi\tan\delta)$$

Above the Arctic or Antarctic circles ($|\varphi| > 90{^\circ} - |\delta|$), this equation has no solution — the Sun never sets (midnight sun) or never rises (polar night).

Step 5

The Atmosphere as a Filter

Beer–Lambert law, air mass, and clear-sky irradiance

So far, we have been working in the vacuum of space. But sunlight must still pass through roughly 100 km of atmosphere before it reaches your panel — and the atmosphere is far from transparent. The same physics that paints the sky blue (Rayleigh scattering) and sunsets red (long slant paths) also attenuates the solar beam.

Air mass

The key geometric quantity is the air mass (AM): the ratio of the actual atmospheric path length to a straight-up vertical path. For a simple plane-parallel atmosphere:

$$\text{AM} \approx \frac{1}{\sin\alpha}$$

When the Sun is directly overhead, $\alpha = 90{^\circ}$ and AM = 1 — the reference condition known as AM1. At sunrise or sunset, $\alpha \rightarrow 0$ and the air mass soars. For large zenith angles where Earth's curvature matters, the Kasten–Young empirical formula handles the correction:

$$\text{AM} = \frac{1}{\sin\alpha + 0.50572\,(\alpha_{\text{deg}} + 6.07995)^{-1.6364}}$$

At a site of elevation $h$ metres, the air above is thinner. A barometric correction accounts for this: $\text{AM}_{\text{corr}} = \text{AM} \times e^{-h/8435}$.

Direct Normal Irradiance

The Beer–Lambert law describes exponential attenuation in any absorbing medium. For a clear sky, the Meinel (1976) empirical approximation gives a satisfyingly simple result:

$$\text{DNI} = G_{\text{ext}} \times 0.7^{\text{AM}^{0.678}}$$

Here DNI is the Direct Normal Irradiance — the beam reaching a surface aimed straight at the Sun. At AM1, roughly 30% of the extraterrestrial power is lost: $\text{DNI} = 1361 \times 0.7 \approx 953$ W/m².

Diffuse and Global Irradiance

Not all scattered photons are "lost" — many reach the ground from the sky dome as diffuse irradiance: $\text{DHI} \approx 0.1\,G_{\text{ext}}\sin\alpha$. The total irradiance on a horizontal surface is the Global Horizontal Irradiance:

$$\text{GHI} = \text{DNI}\sin\alpha + \text{DHI}$$
Note: Real-world atmospheres contain aerosols, humidity, and clouds. This clear-sky model gives a useful upper bound; actual annual yields are typically 60–85% of these predictions, depending on local climate.

Step 6

Irradiance on a Tilted Panel

Angle of incidence from vector geometry

We now arrive at the heart of the problem. A solar panel is not horizontal — it is tilted toward the Sun. How much light it intercepts depends on the cosine law: when the Sun's rays make an angle $\theta$ with the panel's normal vector, the irradiance drops by $\cos\theta$. (Think of a flashlight shining straight at a wall versus at a glancing angle.)

We describe the panel's orientation with two angles:

  • Tilt $\beta$: angle from horizontal (0° = flat, 90° = vertical)
  • Azimuth $\gamma$: compass direction the panel faces (South = 180° in the Northern Hemisphere)

Using unit vectors for the Sun direction $\hat{s}$ and panel normal $\hat{n}$ in the local horizontal coordinate system, the dot product gives:

$$\cos\theta = \sin\alpha\cos\beta + \cos\alpha\sin\beta\cos(A_z - \gamma)$$

This elegant formula packs all the geometry into a single line. For a horizontal panel ($\beta = 0$): $\cos\theta = \sin\alpha$ — the irradiance depends only on how high the Sun is. For a vertical south-facing panel at solar noon: $\cos\theta = \cos\alpha$.

Three components of panel irradiance

A tilted panel collects light from three sources (Liu & Jordan 1963, isotropic sky model):

$$G_T = \underbrace{\text{DNI}\cdot\cos\theta}_{\text{direct beam}} + \underbrace{\text{DHI}\cdot\tfrac{1+\cos\beta}{2}}_{\text{sky diffuse}} + \underbrace{\text{GHI}\cdot\rho\cdot\tfrac{1-\cos\beta}{2}}_{\text{ground-reflected}}$$

where $\rho \approx 0.2$ is the ground albedo (reflectivity). The $(1+\cos\beta)/2$ factor is the panel's view factor to the sky dome.

Step 7

From Irradiance to Electrical Power

Panel efficiency and temperature derating

We now know how much sunlight reaches the panel surface. The final step is converting photons into electrons. But before we reach for equations, it's worth pausing to ask: how does a solar panel actually turn light into electricity?

The answer lies in the photovoltaic effect, first observed by Edmond Becquerel in 1839. When a photon with sufficient energy strikes a silicon atom, it can knock a loosely-bound electron free from the crystal lattice — a quantum-mechanical process described by Einstein's explanation of the photoelectric effect (which won him the Nobel Prize in 1921, not relativity). On its own, this liberates electrons at random. The key engineering insight is to build a p–n junction: a boundary between silicon doped with electron donors (n-type) and silicon doped with electron acceptors (p-type). This junction creates a built-in electric field that sweeps freed electrons in one direction — and that directed flow is electrical current.

This is fundamentally different from solar thermal collectors, which absorb sunlight as heat to warm water or air (or, in concentrated solar power plants, to drive a steam turbine). PV panels skip the heat step entirely: photons become electrons directly. That distinction matters in two ways. First, a cold, bright winter day can be just as productive for PV as a warm summer one — what drives output is light intensity, not temperature. Second, cell heating actually hurts PV efficiency, as we'll see below.

1. Panel area and efficiency:

$$P = A \times \eta \times G_T$$

where $A$ is the panel area in m² and $\eta$ is the conversion efficiency. Modern monocrystalline silicon panels achieve 17–23% under standard test conditions (STC: $G_T = 1000$ W/m², cell temperature = 25°C).

2. Temperature derating: Solar cells lose efficiency as they heat up — an inherent semiconductor effect. The relationship is approximately linear:

$$\eta(T_{\text{cell}}) = \eta_{\text{STC}}\,\left[1 - \gamma_T\,(T_{\text{cell}} - 25{^\circ}\text{C})\right]$$

where $\gamma_T \approx 0.004\,/{^\circ}\text{C}$ for monocrystalline silicon. On a hot day, a panel at 60°C operates at just $1 - 0.004 \times 35 = 86\%$ of its rated efficiency — a significant penalty.

The full instantaneous power equation:

$$P(n,t) = A\,\eta_{\text{STC}}\left[1 - \gamma_T\left(T_{\text{amb}} + \frac{\text{NOCT}-20}{800}G_T - 25\right)\right]G_T$$

where NOCT is the Nominal Operating Cell Temperature (typically 45°C).

Step 8

Annual Energy: Integrating Over a Full Year

Putting all the pieces together

We now have every ingredient. The annual electrical energy yield is the grand integral over all 365 days and 24 hours:

$$E_{\text{annual}} = \int_0^{365}\int_0^{24} P(n,\,t)\;dt\;dn$$

No closed-form solution exists — every link in our chain depends on time in a different way. We evaluate the integral numerically with a midpoint Riemann sum and 30-minute time steps.

Rules of thumb (clear-sky, Northern Hemisphere)

  • Optimal tilt ≈ latitude minus 10–15° (favouring summer, when daylight hours are longest)
  • Optimal azimuth = due South (180° from North) in the Northern Hemisphere
  • Sensitive? Less than you'd think — the annual yield curve is flat within ±15° of the optimum, so small orientation errors are forgiving
  • East/West split: a 50/50 east–west split at 30° tilt loses only ~5% vs optimal south-facing, while better matching afternoon demand peaks
  • Altitude bonus: +1,000 m elevation gains roughly 3–5% in annual irradiance thanks to a thinner atmosphere
